📖 Overview

Kleifarvatn is the largest lake on the Reykjanes peninsula (9.1 km², up to ~97 m deep) and the third-largest in southern Iceland. It sits in a fissure of the Mid-Atlantic Ridge between black lava walls and ash slopes, with a small black-sand beach on the eastern shore. After the 2000 South Iceland earthquakes the lake drained partially through a new fissure and lost about 20% of its surface area before slowly refilling — it remains a quiet, slightly eerie spot, far less visited than the Golden Circle.

🚦 Current Status

🟠 Reykjanes volcanic system on Heightened Unrest (Level 2) — Per the Icelandic Met Office (vedur.is), no active eruption as of April 2026, but ~21 million m³ of magma has accumulated under nearby Svartsengi since the July 2025 Sundhnúkur eruption. Subsidence has been recorded in the Krýsuvík area immediately west of the lake. Check vedur.is and safetravel.is the morning of your visit.

Road 42 (Krýsuvíkurvegur) along the eastern shore is open and paved. The lake itself is open access, no fees, no facilities. Spring conditions: ice has typically broken up by early April but the wind over open water at this elevation can still drop wind-chill below freezing — bring a windproof outer layer.

🚶 Your Visit — Step by Step

1
Pull off at the northern viewpoint on Route 42 Coming from Hafnarfjörður on Road 42, the road climbs over a ridge and the lake suddenly opens out below. There's an unmarked gravel pull-off at the first wide bend with the lake in full view — best orientation shot of the whole basin. Mountains on three sides, lava and ash on the fourth.
⏱️ 5 min · 📍 ~1 km north of the lake's north tip
2
Drive the eastern shore — keep windows down Route 42 hugs the east side for ~5 km. There's almost no traffic. You're driving on a thin tarmac strip between the water and a black ash slope; pull over wherever a wide shoulder appears. The colour contrast (deep blue water, jet-black sand, rust-red rhyolite slopes behind) is the whole point of the stop.
⏱️ 10 min slow drive · 📍 East shore
3
Walk the black-sand beach on the east shore About midway down the eastern shore there's a small parking pull-off and a short scramble down to a black sand beach maybe 100 m long. The sand is volcanic ash washed in from the slopes above. Grab a handful, photograph the pattern of waves on black grit. Almost always empty — this is the quietest "beach" experience you'll get in Iceland.
⏱️ 15-20 min · 📍 Mid east shore, ~63.93°N
4
Continue south to the Hverahlíð / Krýsuvík turn At the south end of the lake the road bends west toward Seltún. The bend itself has a small geothermal seep on the lake's southwestern shore — steam coming straight out of the ground next to the water. Quick photo stop, then push on to Seltún (5 minutes further).
⏱️ 5 min · 📍 South tip of lake
5
Optional: launch a drone (read the section below first) If conditions allow and there's no active TFR, the wide flat lake gives an unusually safe drone launch — no people, no infrastructure, no wildlife concerns. The bird's-eye angle showing the lake shape inside the fissure is the best image you'll get here. Skip if winds exceed your aircraft's rated limit (Reykjanes is windy).
⏱️ 15 min · 📍 East shore pull-off

📷 Photography & Drone

Best compositions:

  • North overlook, wide: 16-24 mm from the first ridge pull-off captures the full basin shape — the lake as a clean ellipse inside dark slopes.
  • Black sand foreground: Get low (50 cm above the sand) on the east beach with a wide lens, water and far shore in the upper third. Mid-morning side light brings out the grain.
  • Reflections: The lake is surprisingly still in early morning before wind picks up. Shoot before 9:00 if you can — Reykjanes wind almost always builds by mid-morning.
  • Tele compression: 100-200 mm from the east shore back across to the rust-red rhyolite slopes on the west side of the lake.

🚁 Drone — likely OK but check for TFRs first. No permanent restriction over Kleifarvatn itself (no national park, no airport overlap), but the entire Reykjanes peninsula is subject to temporary flight restrictions during volcanic events. Check the Icelandic Transport Authority NOTAM page (samgongustofa.is) and the Met Office before flying. If a TFR is active anywhere on the peninsula, do not fly. General Iceland drone rules (Open Category, registration, wind limits) are on the Planning page.

🚗 Getting There

Paved Route 42 (Krýsuvíkurvegur) from Hafnarfjörður south. About 25 minutes from Reykjavík, 35 minutes from KEF airport. Any 2WD rental.

  • Parking: Free, multiple unmarked pull-offs along the east shore.
  • Walk to beach: Less than 1 minute from the mid-shore pull-off.
  • Fees: None.
  • Facilities: None — no toilets, no café, no signage. Use Hafnarfjörður before you set out.

🗺️ Itinerary & Timing

Quick stop on the Reykjanes loop. Pair it directly with Seltún (5 minutes south, same road) — the two are essentially one visit. If you're doing the full Reykjanes day, your sequence is: Kleifarvatn → Seltún → Grindavík → Brimketill → Gunnuhver → Blue Lagoon area.

  • Best light: Early morning for stillness; late afternoon for warm light on the western rhyolite slopes.
  • Crowds: Almost none. Tour buses skip it.
  • Allocate: 30-45 minutes including the beach walk and a few drive-by stops.

⭐ Reviews & Reputation

Google Maps reviewers consistently call Kleifarvatn "underrated" and "the most peaceful spot on Reykjanes." Around 4.6/5. Common praise: solitude, dramatic colour contrast, no tourist infrastructure. Common complaint: "There's nothing to do" — which is exactly the point if you're tired of crowded waterfalls. It's a drive-through and a 10-minute walk, not a destination. Treat it as the calm 30 minutes between geothermal stops.

🎟️ Activities & Providers

Self-guided — no booked activities needed. A few local Reykjavík tour operators include Kleifarfvatn as a brief stop on Reykjanes day tours, but with your own car there's no reason to book one.

⚠️ Safety Notes

  • The shore drops off steeply in places — Kleifarvatn is one of Iceland's deepest lakes. Don't wade.
  • Water is cold year-round; do not enter.
  • The whole peninsula has active volcanic monitoring. If a swarm or eruption begins on the day of your visit, leave immediately and follow safetravel.is alerts.
  • No mobile coverage in some shoreline pockets — let someone know your route.
  • Gusty crosswinds on Route 42 — keep both hands on the wheel.
