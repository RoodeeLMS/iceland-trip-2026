Gunnuhver

📖 Overview

Gunnuhver is Iceland's largest mud pool and the most active part of the Reykjanes high-temperature geothermal area, on the south-western tip of the peninsula a few hundred metres from the Reykjanesviti lighthouse. Steam plumes from the central vent rise 10-20 m on a cold day and the ground rumbles audibly under your feet. Named after Gunna, a 17th-century woman whose ghost (per Icelandic folklore) was lured into the spring by a priest after she'd terrorised the locals — she's said to be still trapped down there. Boardwalks loop around the main vent and the largest mud pots.

🚦 Current Status

🟠 Reykjanes volcanic system on Heightened Unrest (Level 2) — Per the Icelandic Met Office, no eruption underway in April 2026 but ~21 million m³ of magma beneath nearby Svartsengi. Gunnuhver's boardwalks have been damaged by ground heaving and expanding vents multiple times in the past decade — sections may be roped off without notice. Confirm on vedur.is and safetravel.is the morning of your visit.

The site is open access, free, no booking required. Boardwalks and the small parking lot are intact as of recent reports. New vents appear and old ones go quiet on a yearly basis — Gunnuhver is a moving target. April: cold air makes the steam plumes huge and dramatic. Bring eye protection if you're sensitive to sulphur drift. Daylight ~14 hours.

🚶 Your Visit — Step by Step

1
Park at the Gunnuhver lot Free gravel lot at the end of the short access road off Route 425, near the Reykjanesviti lighthouse. Read the sign — it explains the Gunna legend and warns you to stay on the boardwalks.
⏱️ 2 min · 📍 End of access road off Route 425
2
Walk straight to the main steam vent From the lot, the boardwalk leads ~30 m to the central vent — a powerful, continuously roaring fumarole. On a cold morning the plume is enormous and the noise is the closest you'll come to standing next to a jet engine in nature. Stay on the boardwalk; the wind shifts and you may walk through warm steam.
⏱️ 5 min · 📍 Main vent, ~30 m from lot
3
Loop to the large mud pool Follow the boardwalk around to Iceland's largest mud pool — a wide grey-blue cauldron of slow, viscous bubbles. Stand and watch for at least a minute. The biggest plops come irregularly. Best video opportunity here.
⏱️ 8 min · 📍 Mud pool platform, west side of loop
4
Climb to the upper viewpoint A short staircase climbs the slope above the boardwalk to a small viewing platform with a top-down view of the whole Gunnuhver field — vent, mud pool, and the broader rust-red mineral ground. Best wide composition.
⏱️ 5 min · 📍 Upper viewpoint, ~15 m above field
5
Walk 5 minutes to the Reykjanesviti lighthouse From Gunnuhver lot, the path or short drive leads to Iceland's oldest lighthouse on a small hill, with views over the cliffs at Valahnúkamöl and (clear days) the offshore island of Eldey. Quick add-on; pairs naturally with Gunnuhver in one stop.
⏱️ 15-20 min · 📍 Lighthouse, ~800 m from Gunnuhver

📷 Photography & Drone

Best compositions:

  • Backlit steam plume: Position so the sun is behind the main vent. Steam plume glows white against dark sky/hill. 35-85 mm. Best at low sun (early morning or mid-late afternoon in April).
  • Top-down from the upper viewpoint: 24-35 mm wide showing the whole field — orange/rust mineral ground, mud pool, and vent.
  • Mud pop close-up: 70-200 mm tele on a tripod, manual focus on a single bubble, burst mode for the moment of plop.
  • Cold-day plumes: Below 5°C the plume can dwarf a person — best dramatic shots in April mornings before the sun warms the air.

🚁 Drone — DO NOT FLY OVER THE GEOTHERMAL FIELD. Hot, corrosive steam will kill drone motors quickly, and downwash can disturb other visitors. Local site signage discourages drone use at active geothermal sites. The whole Reykjanes peninsula is also subject to TFRs during volcanic activity — check NOTAMs. If you fly, stay over open ground away from the field. General drone rules on the Planning page.

🚗 Getting There

Paved Route 425 to a short signposted access road on the south-western tip of Reykjanes. About 50 minutes from Reykjavík, 25 minutes from KEF airport. Any 2WD rental.

  • Parking: Free gravel lot at the end of the access road.
  • Walk: Less than 1 minute to the main vent boardwalk.
  • Fees: None.
  • Facilities: None on-site. Reykjanesviti area sometimes has portable toilets seasonally — not guaranteed.

🗺️ Itinerary & Timing

Pair with Brimketill (10 min east) and the Reykjanesviti lighthouse (5 min walk). Combined, the three sights are one ~1-hour stop on the western tip of the peninsula.

  • Best light: Early morning (cold air = huge plumes) or late afternoon for warm light on mineral ground.
  • Crowds: Light. Tour buses sometimes drop in for 15 minutes; mostly self-driving travellers.
  • Allocate: ~20 minutes for the Gunnuhver loop alone; ~45 minutes including the lighthouse and Valahnúkamöl cliffs.

⭐ Reviews & Reputation

Tripadvisor and Google reviewers rate it ~4.4/5. Common praise: "much more impressive than expected for a free site," the size of the steam plume, the otherworldly atmosphere. Common complaint: short visit, sulphur smell. A few reviews mention sections of boardwalk being closed when they visited. Honest take: not overrated — for a free 20-minute stop with the largest mud pool in the country, it punches well above its weight.

🎟️ Activities & Providers

Self-guided — no booked activities needed.

⚠️ Safety Notes

  • Stay on the boardwalks at all times. Ground temperatures reach 100°C+ just below the surface. New vents can open without warning.
  • Sulphur dioxide and hydrogen sulphide present. Limit exposure if asthmatic; stay upwind of plumes.
  • If a section of boardwalk is roped off or warning tape is in place, do not cross.
  • Reykjanes volcanic monitoring active — check vedur.is and safetravel.is the morning of your visit.
  • Do not let children or pets off the boardwalk; do not bring strollers (boardwalks have steps).
