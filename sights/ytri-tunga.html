Ytri-Tunga Seal Beach

Day 7
📍 Coordinates64.7961, -23.0789
📅 Trip DayDay 7
📍 Open in Google Maps

📖 Overview

Ytri-Tunga is a low, golden-sand beach on the south side of the Snæfellsnes peninsula and the most reliable place on the entire west coast to see harbor seals hauled out on offshore rocks. There's no cliff, no hike, and no entry fee — you walk 100 m from a small farm parking lot, follow the marked path to the rocky point, and look for seal-shaped lumps on the kelp-covered skerries 50-100 m offshore.

🚦 Current Status

🟢 Open year-round, free. Private farmland — please use the marked path only and do not approach the seals.

April 2026: Pupping season begins late May, so April is pre-pup adults — fewer seals than peak summer but still routinely visible. Wind off Faxaflói bay can be biting; the beach is fully exposed. No reported closures.

🚶 Your Visit — Step by Step

1
Park at the Ytri-Tunga farm lotTurn off Route 54 at the small brown sign for "Selir / Seals." A short gravel track ends at a free dirt lot for ~15 cars right behind the farmhouse. There's a portable toilet and a small information board with a seal-spotting tip sheet.
⏱️ 2 min · 📍 Farm lot, 64.7961, -23.0789
2
Check the tide before you walk downLow tide exposes more skerries and concentrates the seals on the closest rocks; high tide hides the haul-outs and pushes seals offshore. If you can, time the visit for the 2-hour window around low tide (check tide-forecast.com for "Búðir"). At high tide you may still see heads bobbing in the water but it's much harder.
⏱️ 1 min · 📍 At the lot
3
Walk the marked path to the rocky pointFollow the wooden posts ~100 m past the farm onto the beach. Stay on the path — the surrounding meadow is private grazing land. The path ends at a low rocky promontory; this is the seal-viewing spot.
⏱️ 3 min · 📍 ~100m from lot
4
Scan the offshore skerries with binocularsThe seals lie on the dark kelp-covered rocks 50-100 m out. They look like grey-brown lumps until you see one move. Bring binoculars or a telephoto — naked-eye works but is far less rewarding. Be patient: scan slowly across all the rocks left to right twice before deciding "no seals."
⏱️ 15-25 min · 📍 Rocky point
5
Stay 50 m back, talk quietlyPer the farm's posted signage and Icelandic Seal Center guidance, do not wade out toward the rocks and do not throw stones, shout, or run on the beach. Stressed seals abandon haul-outs and may not return for the rest of the day — bad for you, worse for the next visitors and the seals.
⏱️ — · 📍 Whole visit
6
Walk the small beach for shells and birdsUnusually for Snæfellsnes the sand here is pale yellow, not black — a thin shell layer over basalt. Eider ducks, oystercatchers and purple sandpipers feed along the wrack line. Five minutes of beachcombing rounds out the stop.
⏱️ 5-10 min · 📍 Along the sand

📷 Photography & Drone

  • Tele compression for seals: 300-400 mm equivalent minimum. Anything wider and the seals are lentil-sized in the frame. Crouch low to flatten the angle.
  • Best light: Morning (sun behind you to the east) — afternoon backlights the rocks against the bay.
  • Wide environment shot: 24-35 mm of the yellow beach with Snæfellsjökull on the horizon (north-west) makes a good "place" image without the seals.

🚫 Drone: Do not fly here. The whole point of the site is wildlife viewing, and drone noise scatters seal haul-outs (per Icelandic Seal Center field guidance). Ytri-Tunga is also private farmland — owners can refuse drone use. For general Iceland drone rules see the Planning page drone section.

🚗 Getting There

Paved Route 54, then a short gravel track. Any rental car. ~15 minutes east of Búðir / Búðakirkja.

  • Parking: Free farm lot, ~15 spaces. Can fill in summer; in April you'll have it.
  • Walk: 100 m flat path from car to viewing point.
  • Fees: None. A small donation box at the info board supports path upkeep.

🗺️ Itinerary & Timing

  • Allocate: 30-40 min total including the walk. Don't budget more — seals or no seals, you'll be done.
  • Best time: Around low tide, morning light. Check tide tables for Búðir.
  • Combine with: First stop on the Day 7 Snæfellsnes loop heading west — Búðakirkja (15 min west) is the natural next stop, then Arnarstapi.
  • Skip if: High tide and rough seas, or if you have less than 5 hours total for the peninsula.

⭐ Reviews & Reputation

Google Maps rating ~4.4 (3,000+ reviews). Praised as the easiest, no-fuss seal viewing in Iceland; complaints almost always boil down to "no seals when we visited" — which means the visitor came at high tide, in a storm, or did not bring binoculars. Honest take: set expectations to "scan rocks 100 m offshore for grey lumps," not "swim with seals." With binoculars and decent tide it's a guaranteed quick win; without, it can feel anticlimactic.

🎟️ Activities & Providers

Self-guided — no booked activities needed.

⚠️ Safety Notes

  • Slippery seaweed on the rocks — sturdy shoes, watch your footing.
  • Do not wade out toward the seals — cold water, hidden channels, and you'll empty the haul-out.
  • Strong wind off the bay — wind layers essential even on a "calm" day.
  • Private farmland; respect fences and the marked path.
