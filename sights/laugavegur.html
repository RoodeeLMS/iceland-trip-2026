Laugavegur Main Street

Day 1
📍 Coordinates64.1446, -21.9306
📅 Trip DayDay 1
📍 Open in Google Maps🥾 Walking directions▶️ Watch on YouTube

📖 Overview

Laugavegur ("Wash Road" — historically the route women took to wash clothes in the hot springs of Laugardalur) is Reykjavik's main shopping and dining street, running about 1.5 km from Hlemmur Square in the east down to Bankastraeti in the west, where it becomes the central downtown spine. Most of the city's restaurants, design shops, bookstores, gift shops, and cafes are on Laugavegur or one of its side streets. It's the place you'll naturally end up for any meal in Reykjavik.

🚦 Current Status

🟢 Open and active. Laugavegur's central section has been gradually pedestrianised — significant stretches are car-free year-round as of 2025-2026. Shops are mostly open Mon-Sat 10:00-18:00, Sun 12:00-17:00. Restaurants run later. Most accept card; many shops process VAT refunds for non-EU visitors over ISK 6,000.

April 2026 conditions: Sidewalks can be slushy or icy in the morning. The street is well-lit and active until late.

🚶 Your Visit — Step by Step

1
Start at Hlemmur Square — eat at the food hall Begin at the eastern end at Hlemmur Square. The Hlemmur Mathöll (food hall) inside the old bus station is the best cheap-to-mid eat in central Reykjavik — fish-and-chips, Vietnamese banh mi, Italian, falafel, ramen. Mains ฿450-700. Way better value than the tourist restaurants further down. Use this as a lunch base.
⏱️ 30-45 min · 📍 Hlemmur Square, east end of Laugavegur
2
Walk west down Laugavegur — browse the shops Walk down the street toward Bankastraeti. Notable stops along the way: 66°North (Icelandic outerwear, expensive but quality), Geysir (Icelandic-design clothing), Eymundsson (the main bookstore — great for Icelandic literature in English), Skuli Craft Bar area, design shops, and dozens of gift / wool / souvenir shops of varying quality. Stick to 66°North, Geysir, and the Handknitting Association branches for genuine Icelandic-made items.
⏱️ 30-60 min · 📍 Laugavegur central, ~800m walk
3
Coffee or pastry break at a local bakery Braud & Co. (Frakkastigur, side street off Laugavegur) is an Instagram-famous bakery — sourdough cinnamon rolls, kanelsnudur, dark Icelandic rye bread. Cheap by Reykjavik standards (~฿200 for a pastry). Sandholt on Laugavegur itself is the older, equally good alternative with sit-down seating. Either is a fine mid-walk stop.
⏱️ 15-20 min · 📍 Frakkastigur or Laugavegur
4
Detour up Skolavordustigur to Hallgrimskirkja About halfway down Laugavegur, Skolavordustigur (Rainbow Street) branches off to the south. Take the detour up to Hallgrimskirkja and back — see the rainbow-street and hallgrimskirkja pages. This is the natural way to combine all three.
⏱️ 60-90 min round trip including church
5
Continue west to Bankastraeti and the harbour Past the Skolavordustigur junction, Laugavegur becomes Bankastraeti and continues to the Old Town and Tjornin pond. Most of the heavier-hitting restaurants for dinner cluster here and one block south. Notable: Icelandic Street Food (cheap lamb soup with free refills, ฿525), Cafe Loki (traditional Icelandic), Reykjavik Roasters (the best coffee in Iceland, several locations).
⏱️ 20-40 min · 📍 West Laugavegur / Bankastraeti
6
Dinner pick — book ahead For Day 1 dinner, the well-rated mid-range options on or just off Laugavegur include Messinn (fish, ฿900-1,400 mains), Snaps Bistro (French-Icelandic, ฿1,200+), Kol Restaurant (upscale, ฿1,800+), or the cheaper Icelandic Street Food. Book Messinn and Snaps a few days ahead — they fill on weekends.
⏱️ Plan ahead

📷 Photography & Drone

  • Street life: 35-50 mm prime, candid style. The painted facades and colourful graffiti are the best subjects, not the people.
  • Best at dusk: When shop lights come on but the sky still holds blue. April: ~20:30-21:30.
  • Skolavordustigur junction: The best wide composition is from the corner where Skolavordustigur meets Laugavegur — you can frame the rainbow street with Hallgrimskirkja's tower in one shot.

🚫 Drone: No. Central Reykjavik is BIRK controlled airspace and Laugavegur is a busy public street — drone flight here requires explicit clearance per the Icelandic Transport Authority. General drone rules on the Planning page.

🚗 Getting There

  • Walk: The street IS the centre — every downtown hotel is on it or within 5 minutes of it.
  • Parking: P1/P2 zones, ~ISK 220-380/hr (~฿55-95). Free Sundays. Most central section is pedestrianised, so park on side streets.
  • Bus: Hlemmur Square (east end) is the main Straeto hub.
  • Fees: None. Open street.

🗺️ Itinerary & Timing

  • Best time: Late afternoon into evening. Shops open until 18:00, then restaurants take over.
  • Combine with: Hallgrimskirkja + Skolavordustigur (south detour), Sun Voyager + Harpa (continue north past Bankastraeti). The classic Day 1 walking loop.
  • Allocate: 2-4 hours including a meal. Easy to spend longer if you actually shop.

⭐ Reviews & Reputation

Tripadvisor and Google reviews are universally positive — Laugavegur consistently appears in "top 10 things to do in Reykjavik" lists, mostly because everyone naturally ends up here. It's the city's spine.

Praised: Walkable, photogenic, dense cluster of restaurants and shops, Hlemmur Mathöll for cheap food, Braud & Co bakery, the design shops on Skolavordustigur.

Common complaints: Eye-watering prices (a dinner main can hit ฿1,500+), tourist-trap gift shops mixed in with the genuine ones, slippery sidewalks in winter.

Honest take: Not really a "sight" — it's where you naturally spend your time in Reykjavik for food, browsing, and walking. Don't try to "do" Laugavegur; just plan to eat dinner here, do some shopping, and walk it twice. Stick to Geysir / 66°North / Handknitting Association for shopping if you want quality and skip the cheap puffin-magnet shops.

🎟️ Activities & Providers

Self-guided. Several walking tours (Free Walking Tour Reykjavik, CityWalk Reykjavik) cover Laugavegur as part of a 2-hour central loop — tip-based, no booking needed. Not necessary but good if you want context.

⚠️ Safety Notes

Very safe. Reykjavik has very low street crime by European standards. The only issues are slippery sidewalks in winter/early spring (carry small grip covers if you have them) and the inevitable cluster of drunk locals and tourists on Friday and Saturday nights after midnight — harmless but loud.
