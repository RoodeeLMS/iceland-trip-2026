📖 Overview

Seljalandsfoss is a 60 m glacier-fed waterfall plunging off a former sea cliff on the south coast, ~120 km from Reykjavik. Its claim to fame is the cave-like recess behind the falling water — normally one of the only walk-behind waterfalls in Iceland that you can complete a full 360° loop around. It's the first major waterfall on the south coast Ring Road drive and almost always the busiest.

🚦 Current Status

🔴 WALK-BEHIND PATH CLOSED — April 2026
Per safetravel.is active pin (verbatim): "The path behind Seljalandsfoss is closed due to ice and icicle." The path that loops around the back of the waterfall is shut for the entire spring freeze-thaw window because of falling icicles and ice on the rocks. Do not cross any closure tape.

What's still open: The front viewing area, the boardwalks and trails along the base, and the side stair-trail south to a higher viewpoint. The waterfall itself is fully visible and just as photogenic from the front — most of the iconic backlit-rainbow shots are taken from the boardwalk approach, not from behind. You aren't missing the headline view, just the loop.

April 2026 conditions: Spray is heavy and wind-blown — wear a proper rain shell and protect cameras. Ground is still icy in spots; microspikes are useful but not mandatory if you stay on the boardwalk. Daylight is ~14 hours so timing is flexible.

🚶 Your Visit — Step by Step

1
Arrive & pay parking — main lot off Route 249 Turn off Route 1 onto Route 249 and the lot is signed within 500 m. Parking is ISK 800 ≈ ฿200, paid at the machine or via the Parka app, valid all day. Use the public toilets here (ISK 200 ≈ ฿50) — there are none farther on.
⏱️ 5 min · 📍 Main lot, 63.6156, -19.9886
2
Walk the boardwalk to the front of the falls From the lot it's a flat ~250 m path on gravel/boardwalk to the base. Stop at the wooden viewing platform directly in front for the classic head-on shot. Put on your rain shell now — by the time you're 30 m from the base the spray will be soaking everything.
⏱️ 10 min · 📍 Front viewing area, ~250m from lot
3
Approach the closure tape — see (but don't enter) the cave Continue right toward where the walk-behind path normally begins. The path is taped/signed off due to ice. From the closure point you can still see up into the cavern and shoot the curtain of water from a tight side angle — arguably a better composition than the usual midpoint shot. Respect the tape; rangers actively enforce it.
⏱️ 5 min · 📍 Path entrance (closed), north side of falls
4
Climb the south-side stair trail for the elevated view On the opposite (south) side of the falls a metal staircase climbs the cliff to a higher viewpoint. Conditions permitting (check for ice — handrails help), this puts you level with the upper third of the falls and gives a clean profile shot toward the river plain. ~80 steps up.
⏱️ 10 min · 📍 South-side stair, ~50m from front platform
5
Walk north along the cliff base toward Gljufrabui From the front platform, head north (right when facing the falls) along the obvious path that runs along the base of the cliff. It's flat, ~500 m / 5-7 minutes, passing two minor waterfalls along the way. This delivers you straight to Gljufrabui — see its dedicated page.
⏱️ 7 min walk · 📍 Cliff-base trail, leads to Gljufrabui
6
Dry off, swap lenses, refuel Back at the lot there's a small food truck/snack stand (cash & card, hours vary in spring). Use the time to wipe down camera gear with a microfiber — spray on lenses dries to spots fast. Total visit including Gljufrabui: 60-90 minutes.
⏱️ 10 min · 📍 Back at main lot

📷 Photography & Drone

Best compositions:

  • Classic head-on, golden hour: Stand on the front boardwalk, 16-24 mm wide. Best in late afternoon — the sun arcs around to the west and backlights the falls from 17:00 onward, often producing a rainbow.
  • Tight curtain shot from the closed path entrance: A 35-50 mm tighter frame from the side, just shy of the closure tape. Captures the texture of the water against the dark basalt with no crowds.
  • Elevated profile from south stairs: 50-85 mm from the upper platform compresses the falls against the cliff face.
  • ND filter for 1/2-1 sec long exposures to silk the water — but the wind makes tripod use tricky; brace against the boardwalk railing.
  • Gear protection is the #1 issue here, not composition. Lens cloth in pocket, rain cover, blower bulb at the car.

🚫 Drones discouraged at Seljalandsfoss — heavy crowds, narrow valley with unpredictable spray-driven turbulence, and on-site signage requesting no drones near the falls (per Guide to Iceland and Iceland Photo Tours, 2026). Treat the immediate area as no-fly. For general Iceland drone rules see the Drone section on the Planning page.

🚗 Getting There

Paved Route 249 directly off Route 1, 120 km from Reykjavik (~1h 45min drive). Any car, no 4x4 needed. The lot is large but fills mid-day — arrive before 10:00 or after 16:00 for easy parking.

  • Parking: ISK 800 ≈ ฿200, all-day. Pay at the machine or via the Parka app.
  • Toilets: ISK 200 ≈ ฿50 at the lot (only ones for several km).
  • Walk to main viewpoint: ~3-5 min on flat boardwalk.
  • No entry fee to the falls.

🗺️ Itinerary & Timing

Star sight on Day 3. Pair with Gljufrabui (5 min walk away) as a single 60-90 minute stop, then continue to Skogafoss (~30 min drive east).

  • Best light: Late afternoon (16:00-18:00 in early April) — sun is behind you and may produce a rainbow in the spray.
  • Crowds: Tour buses arrive 11:00-15:00. Early morning is dramatically quieter and the falls face northwest so morning light is flat anyway — consider a brief morning stop and return for sunset if logistics allow.
  • Combine with: Gljufrabui (always), Skogafoss (~30 min east), Seljavallalaug pool turnoff (we won't swim, but the drive past is scenic).
  • Time budget: 45-60 min for Seljalandsfoss alone, +20 min for Gljufrabui.

⭐ Reviews & Reputation

Google rating ~4.7 (60,000+ reviews). Universally loved when conditions are good — the walk-behind is the headline that drives most rave reviews. With the path closed, expectations need to be calibrated: this is still one of the most striking waterfalls in Iceland, but you're getting the front-of-house experience only.

Honest take: Not overrated, but the walk-behind closure does take away the "wow" people are expecting. The fix: come knowing that, treat it as a 30-45 min photo stop, and don't oversell it to the group beforehand. Most common complaint year-round is parking crowds and getting soaked unprepared — both fixable.

🎟️ Activities & Providers

Self-guided — no booked activities needed. Standard stop on every south coast bus tour from Reykjavik; with our rental car there's no reason to book one.

⚠️ Safety Notes

  • Walk-behind path is closed — falling ice and icicles. Per safetravel.is: "The path behind Seljalandsfoss is closed due to ice and icicle." Do not cross the tape.
  • Boardwalk and front area can be slick from spray and refreezing — walk carefully, especially in the morning.
  • Heavy spray will soak unprotected cameras and phones in seconds.
  • Wind in the open river plain can be strong; secure hats and tripod legs.
  • Children should be hand-held near the front platform — wet rocks and the river drop-off are slippery.
