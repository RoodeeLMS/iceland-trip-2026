Deildartunguhver

Day 6★ Priority
📍 Coordinates64.6636, -21.4081
📅 Trip DayDay 6 (West / Silver Circle)
📍 Open in Google Maps🥾 Walking directions

📖 Overview

Deildartunguhver is the highest-flow hot spring in Europe — ~180 litres per second of water at 97°C / 207°F erupts continuously from a fissure in the basalt. The output is so large it heats homes and greenhouses across two towns (Borgarnes and Akranes) via the longest hot-water pipeline in Iceland. On site you walk a short boardwalk past steam vents and the open boiling pools. Adjacent is Krauma Spa (built 2017), which mixes the spring's water with cold glacial melt for a bathing complex. Our group is not swimming — Krauma is mentioned here only as a skip / reference.

🚦 Current Status

🟢 Open year-round, free walk-up site, no closures — Per krauma.is: the spring viewing area is free and accessible all year. Krauma Spa adjacent is also year-round.

April 2026 conditions: Cold ambient air + 97°C water = enormous billowing steam clouds, the most photogenic time of year for this stop. Boardwalks may be icy from condensed steam refreezing — watch your footing. The site is small and exposed; if it's blowing horizontal sleet, the visit shrinks to 5 minutes from the parking lot edge.

🚶 Your Visit — Step by Step

1
Park at the free Deildartunguhver lot Free paved lot directly off Route 50, signposted. Krauma Spa shares the access road but has its own parking; the spring boardwalk lot is the one closer to the steaming hillside. Restrooms inside Krauma's reception (if you're using them, technically you should be a customer — the cafe/coffee at Krauma is the polite workaround).
⏱️ 2 min · 📍 Free lot, Rd 50
2
Walk the boardwalk past the main vents A short railed boardwalk (~100 m total) runs alongside the spring fissure. Stop at the first vent to feel the heat radiating off the rock. The water is genuinely 97°C — close to boiling. Do NOT step off the boardwalk; surface crusts elsewhere on the slope can collapse into superheated water (this is the standard Icelandic geothermal hazard).
⏱️ 5 min · 📍 Main boardwalk, ~50 m from lot
3
Look for the open boiling pool at the end of the boardwalk The far end opens onto the largest visible pool — clear water rolling and visibly boiling. In April cold this is wrapped in a constant steam plume. Photographic gold for backlit / silhouette compositions if the sun is low.
⏱️ 5 min · 📍 End of boardwalk
4
Notice the geothermal pipeline & tomato greenhouses Look uphill — the silver insulated pipes carry the spring water 64 km to Akranes, the longest hot-water pipeline in Iceland. Across the road, geothermally heated greenhouses grow tomatoes and other produce year-round (some sold at Krauma's restaurant). Quick context stop, ~2 min.
⏱️ 2 min · 📍 View from boardwalk back toward road
5
(Skip) Krauma Spa The bathing complex next door opened in 2017 and mixes the spring water with glacial melt for soaking. Adult entry ~ISK 4,990 ≈ ฿1,250. Our group does not swim — note it as a "skip" reference. The Krauma cafe/restaurant is open to non-bathers if you want a coffee with the steam plume in view.
⏱️ Skip · 📍 Krauma building, adjacent

📷 Photography & Drone

Best compositions:

  • Backlit steam plume: Shoot toward the low sun (early morning or late afternoon) so the steam glows and the foreground stays moody. 35-85 mm works best — wide loses the impact, super-tele compresses it too flat.
  • Detail of the boiling pool: 70-200 mm tele on the surface roll. 1/500 sec freezes the boiling pattern; 1/15 sec turns it abstract.
  • Pipeline as foreground: Use the silver insulated pipes as a leading line into the steam — gives the shot a sense of scale and tells the geothermal-heating story in one frame.
  • April advantage: Cold air = 5-10x more visible steam than summer photos. This is the right season for this site.

🚁 Drone: No site-specific ban posted, but the site is small, partially within the Krauma commercial property boundary, and other visitors are usually within the small viewing area — keep flights short, low, and away from the spa building. Mind the steam plume (warm rising air messes with rotor lift). For general Iceland drone law see the Drone section on the Planning page.

🚗 Getting There

~1.5 hr from Reykjavik via Ring Road then Route 50. All paved, any 2WD rental fine.

  • Parking: Free at the spring lot (Krauma has its own).
  • Walk to boardwalk: ~50 m flat.
  • Entry: Free for the spring. Krauma Spa: ~ISK 4,990 ≈ ฿1,250 adult (skip).
  • Restrooms: Inside Krauma reception (be a cafe customer).

🗺️ Itinerary & Timing

Quick stop, ~15 min on the boardwalk plus 5 min for parking and orientation. Pair with Hraunfossar / Barnafoss to the north (~30 min drive) on Day 6's West loop.

  • Best light: Cold mornings = biggest steam plumes. Late afternoon backlight is the most dramatic.
  • Crowds: Light. A handful of tour buses pass through but the boardwalk is short so turnover is fast.
  • Combine with: Hraunfossar + Barnafoss (~30 km north), Reykholt historical site (~10 min east, Snorri Sturluson connection), Krauma cafe.
  • Allocate 15-20 min total. Don't budget more — it's small.

⭐ Reviews & Reputation

Tripadvisor ~4.0/5. Reviewers consistently call it interesting but small — the praise centres on the steam plume and the geothermal-engineering story; the complaints are about it being a 10-minute stop rather than a destination. Krauma Spa adjacent gets generally positive reviews from bathers but is irrelevant to a non-swimming visit.

Honest take: Matches expectations. Don't expect a Geysir / Strokkur experience — there's no eruption show, just a continuously boiling fissure. The "highest flow in Europe" stat is the hook, and it's genuinely impressive once you understand 180 L/s. Worth a 15 min stop, not a 1 hr one.

🎟️ Activities & Providers

Self-guided for the spring itself. Optional (skip): Krauma Geothermal Spa, ~ISK 4,990 ≈ ฿1,250 adult, no advance booking required, open year-round. Group is not swimming — listed for reference only.

⚠️ Safety Notes

  • 97°C water — stay on the boardwalk. Stepping off can break through a thin surface crust into scalding water/steam. Standard Icelandic geothermal area rule.
  • Boardwalk surfaces can be icy in April from condensed steam refreezing — go slow.
  • Keep cameras / phones on wrist straps near the steam — moisture and lens fog are constant.
