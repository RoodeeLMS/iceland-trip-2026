📖 Overview

A 1973 US Navy Douglas Super DC-3 (C-117D) that ran out of fuel and crash-landed on the black sand plain of Sólheimasandur. No fatalities — the crew walked away and the wreck has sat on the sand ever since, stripped of wings, engines, and paint by decades of salt spray and Icelandic wind.

Over the past decade it became one of Iceland's most-Instagrammed locations, helped along by music videos (Justin Bieber, Sigur Rós), a Star Wars-esque aesthetic, and the desolate black-on-black composition. It's pure post-apocalyptic photography — and that's the only real reason to go.

🚦 Current Status

Open year-round. The wreck is on private land; the landowner permits walking access but NO driving on the sand plain since 2016 (after too many stuck cars and injured tourists).

The shuttle bus runs seasonally — typically May-Sep. In April 2026 you will almost certainly be walking both ways.

April 2026 specifics: The walk is flat and easy but brutally exposed — no shelter from wind, no landmarks, no shade. Average completion time ~2 hours round trip, longer in strong wind.

🚶 Your Visit — Step by Step

1
Park at the official lot on Route 1 Signed "Sólheimasandur Plane Wreck Parking" at approximately 63.4914°N, 19.3641°W. Free parking. Toilets at the parking lot. DO NOT attempt to drive onto the sand — you will get stuck, there is no cell signal, and there is a steep fine.
⏱️ 2 min · 📍 Route 1 pull-off
2
Walk the 4 km gravel track south toward the coast Straight, flat, featureless. Follow the obvious track (there is only one). Dress warmly — there is zero wind shelter for the entire walk. Bring water even in cold weather; people underestimate the exposure.
⏱️ ~45-60 min one way · 📍 4 km flat gravel
3
Arrive at the wreck The DC-3 sits alone on the black plain. Walk around it, inside it, underneath it. Surfaces are sharp — watch hands and feet. The interior is gutted; stand where the pilots sat. The wings are long gone; the fuselage is what remains.
⏱️ 15-30 min on-site · 📍 63.4593°N, 19.3645°W
4
Walk back the 4 km the same way Same path, no scenic alternative. On a bad-wind day this is where people underestimate the energy cost. Allow 45-60 min return.
⏱️ 45-60 min · 📍 same track

📷 Photography & Drone

Best compositions:

  • Wide environmental shot: 16-24mm, stand 30-50m away, position the wreck centered on a featureless horizon. The vastness of the sand plain is the story.
  • Inside the fuselage looking out: 16mm wide, put the ribcage of the aircraft frame in silhouette against the sky through the missing tail section.
  • Person-for-scale: Place someone in a colored jacket 20m in front of the wreck — shows the "lonely end of the world" mood.
  • Blue hour / astro: Star photography and aurora foregrounds are the other reason people come. Plan for after sunset if the forecast is clear and Kp is elevated — adds 90+ min to the visit but the shots are unique.

Best time of day: Overcast flat light is actually ideal — removes harsh shadows and suits the bleak aesthetic. Golden hour works too but the low April sun creates strong front/back-light contrast. Avoid midday if possible.

🚁 Drone: Recreational drone use is permitted here (not inside a national park, no signage prohibiting). Excellent aerial subject — tiny speck on vast plain. Watch wind limits for DJI Neo 2 (8 m/s rating); south coast gusts routinely exceed that. See Planning → Drone Usage for general rules.

🚗 Getting There

Parking lot directly off Ring Road (Route 1), between Skógafoss and Vík. No fee, no booking, open year-round.

  • Parking: free, large gravel lot, signed "Sólheimasandur Plane Wreck Parking"
  • Toilets: yes, at the parking lot
  • Walk to wreck: 4 km each way, ~2 hours round trip on flat gravel track
  • No shuttle in April — shuttle service typically May-September only
  • No entry fee
  • Closest fuel/food: Skógafoss N1 (10 min west) or Vík N1 (15 min east)

🗺️ Itinerary & Timing

Day 3 timing caveat: Day 3 is already your most packed day (Seljalandsfoss, Gljúfrabúi, Skógafoss, Kvernufoss, Reynisfjara, Dyrhólaey, Vík). Adding 2 hours here is aggressive. Realistic options:

  • Option A — skip on Day 3, visit Day 5 instead. Day 5 is the buffer "drive west" day — much more flex to slot in a 2-hour walk in the morning.
  • Option B — fit it on Day 3 by dropping something else. Trade: skip Kvernufoss (walk-behind) or Skógar Museum to free up ~1.5-2 hours.
  • Option C — sunset/blue hour on Day 3 or Day 5. April has long dusk; walk out around 18:00 for golden light at the wreck, walk back in blue hour with headlamps.

Timing advice: Plan for 2.5 hours total minimum (45 min walk + 30 min at wreck + 45 min walk + buffer). Add 30-60 min in bad wind. Check the forecast page before committing — if sustained wind >15 m/s, skip; the walk becomes miserable.

⭐ Reviews & Reputation

Google Maps rating: ~4.5/5 — heavily visited and mostly positive, but reviews are sharply split.

What visitors praise: Unique atmosphere. Impressive photography subject. The walk itself has a meditative quality. Empty even in summer once you walk past the first 500m.

Common complaints: "The walk is 4 KM EACH WAY!" (not signposted prominently — many tourists start walking not realizing). Brutal wind exposure. Disappointment at the wreck being smaller than expected in photos.

Honest assessment: Overrated as a "sight", underrated as a photography assignment. If you do not care about photographs, skip it entirely — there is nothing to see that a 2-minute Google image search would not give you. If you do care about photographs and want the shot, the walk is part of the experience.

🎟️ Activities & Providers

Self-guided. No booked activities, no tours, no entry fee.

⚠️ Safety Notes

  • Never drive onto the sand. The landowner has banned vehicles since 2016. Cars get stuck, tow recovery is expensive, and fines apply.
  • Wind exposure: ZERO shelter for the entire walk. Dress for the wind chill, not the air temperature. On a 5°C / 20 m/s day the effective temp is well below zero.
  • Bring water: 2 hours of walking with no services.
  • Navigation: Follow the obvious gravel track. In heavy fog (rare but possible), turn back — it is easy to get disoriented on the featureless plain.
  • Sharp metal: The wreck is unrestored and stripped. Edges are sharp. Watch hands, feet, and children.
  • Cell signal: spotty to non-existent on the walk itself. Tell someone you are going.
  • In an emergency: dial 112.
